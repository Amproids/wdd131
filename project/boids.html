How It Works

What are Boids?

The Boids model, created by Craig Reynolds in 1986, is a classic example of artificial life, simulating the flocking behavior seen in birds and other animal groups. The term "boid" refers to a "bird-like object," an autonomous agent that follows a set of simple rules. What makes Boids so fascinating is that complex flocking patterns emerge from the interactions of these individual agents, each acting independently based on local information. This is known as emergent behavior: the whole is greater than the sum of its parts. The Boids simulation beautifully illustrates how order can arise from decentralized control.

In our model, the boids will have a limit to their turn rate, and will scale directly with their speed. Meaning the speed wont affect the simulation, it will simply slow it down. Like slo-motion.

The Three Rules

Separation

Boids will steer away from nearby boids and obsticles.

Alignment

Boids will try to align their steering direction with the direction of neighboring boids.

Cohesion

Boids try to fly towards the center of mass of neighboring boids.

Applications

The principles behind boids have found applications in various fields:

  • Computer Graphics and Animation
  • Crowd Behavior Simulation
  • Autonomous Systems and Robotics
  • Traffic Flow Modeling
